Music 27 Assignment 2

Posted on 2016-12-06

For this assignment, we are looking at Whitehead: Toura. This is a piece in In 27 Pieces: The Hilary Hahn Encores, an Grammy-winning album by Hilary Hahn and Cory Smythe. In this album, Hilary commissions 27 small pieces from 27 composers around the globe and Toura is composed by Gillian Whitehead, a New Zealand composer. Like most other pieces in this album, this one is performed mainly with the the violin for the majority and piano for the rest.

Since this is an open project, I’ve decided to look at this project from a programmer’s perspective, which is to visualize this piece of music.

Tech Approaches

since this is a music course, I’ll quickly cover this part.
To visualize any piece of music, one needs to use the following process:

  1. Slice the music into small pieces.
  2. Perform a Fast Fourier transform on these slices to extract the frequency components
  3. Use these components to draw something

Good programmers know what to write. Great ones know what to rewrite (and reuse).
Eric S. Raymond in The Mail Must Get Through

After understanding the techie part of the work while also knowing from my childhood that so many music players do the visualization so I can definitely find some written code on the internet, I started to Google hard.

Visualizations

After some digging on the web and code tweaking, I adapted two raw projects and tailored them to my needs.

The Ring Visualizing

The first project I came across was by Abhinav Kumar. He wrote a ring visualizer and open-sourced it. I downloaded his code and ran with it, with parameters tuned so the our track gets played and visualized and all the sizes fit. The result is marvelous:

In this visualization, the different parts of the ring represent different frequencies, and the width of the ring represents the amount of the sound in that frequency. From watching it, I concluded two things.

On the one hand, the spectrum of piano sound and violin sound are vastly different.

  • The visualization of violin sound:
  • The visualization of piano sound:

We can easily notice that the violin sound seems much more smooth and full, with the whole ring having a similar width, while the piano sound seems scattered into peak points shown by the huge difference present in the width of the ring. The piano ring is so deformed that is almost stop looking like a circle.

On the other hand, although this visualization demonstrates the changes distribution of the frequencies, it does not record any information over time. We also want something that shows the changes in the frequency distribution, which leads to the second visualization.

Narratives Visualization

After staring at Google for about two hours, I finally found this project by an Interaction Designer, Matthias Dittrich. Here’s his explanation of the visualization:

Narrative 2.0 visualises music. The different music tracks were segmented into single channels. These channels are shown in a fanlike structure where lines move away from the centre with the flow of time. The angle of the line changes according to the volume, and when reaching a higher level, the channel becomes highlighted in orange. The visualisation does not necessarily return exact information, however, the order and uniformity of the song can still be perceived.

So basically this visualization has multiple lines that fan out from a starting point, and the lines alter in direction and color when the volume at the frequency the line represents reaches a threshold. I didn’t care for this blunt approach, so I reprogrammed it so that the direction and color changes gradually by how much the volume differs from the average volume. By making the threshold dynamic and changes gradual, my version of the visualization is more smooth and a more accurate reflection of the music, which by nature has an analogue property.

Here’s the full video of the visualization with my real time notes:

The information I can extract from this is that this piece of the music is generally a quiet and smooth one. The majority of the sound is concentrated in the lower part of the range, and the higher pitches are generally in the form of short bursts.

Analysis

The name of the piece is actually Tōrua, which I believe means “change” or “opposite” in Maori, one of the less popular languages used in New Zealand. Just like the name suggests, I think this piece has two very opposite sides / components, one being the violin, the other being the piano. The sound properties of these instruments are discussed above in the ring video. If you recall the real-time comments I left in the ring visualization, you’d know that I think this piece consists of 3 phases. Below is my interpretation of them.

First Phase: Solos 0:00 - 1:35

This phase started with a few low notes by the piano, then the violin starts at a higher pitch and also a completely different style. It feels like we have a person who’s starting to play some moody and heavy piano, then the other person immediately decides against it and play the violin with a totally different style. Then the violin becomes the major instrument here and we have the same story with the piano tries to make its comeback but never could. At around 0:45, you can hear the repeated notes made with the piano, and it feels just like that person is making some noise as an attempt to grab attention, like the music teacher in a primary school.

Second Phase: Competition 1:35 - 2:50

This phase also started with some piano marks. We also have the violin kicks in, but this time in a different manner: they agree with each other. They have the same pitches and the same rhythms. However, although they seem to be in agreement, they both try to ‘outplay’ the other. You can tell by the vibrato of the violin, and the rapid bursts of the note made by the piano.

Third Phase: Collaboration 2:50 - 4:00

In this phase, both parties are truly in agreement. If we continue to use the analogy above, it’d be like they are tired of fighting each other and now decides to work with each other.

Conclusion

Like the name suggests, when the phase shifts, the relationship between the two opposite parties also changes in a this interesting manner. In the beginning, I didn’t quite like this piece because it lacks the harmony in the first phase. It simply sounded weird and not musical. Then after spotting the hidden shifts in style, I realized how cleverly constructed this piece is:
It’s a story about meeting the difference, fighting the difference and eventually embraceing the difference.
